## Easy (1 per company = 3 total)

Easy questions ask about a single, clearly stated fact that can be found by glancing at the report — no calculation or inference required. Source is cited.

**Example:**

> **Q:** What was Netflix's total revenue for fiscal year 2023?
>
> **A:** Netflix reported total revenue of $33.7 billion for fiscal year 2023, as stated in the consolidated statements of operations in their 2023 10-K filing.
>
> **Supporting Facts:**
> Source: Netflix 10-K FY2023, pg. 57

---

## Medium (1 per company = 3 total)

Medium questions cannot be answered from a single line in the report. They require searching across multiple sections, performing calculations, or inferring figures not directly stated.

**Example:**

> **Q:** What was Netflix's operating margin for fiscal year 2023, and how did it change from the prior year?
>
> **A:** Netflix's operating margin for fiscal year 2023 was 20.6%, calculated by dividing operating income ($7.0 billion) by total revenue ($33.7 billion). This is an improvement from 17.8% in fiscal year 2022 ($5.6 billion operating income / $31.6 billion revenue), reflecting margin expansion driven by cost discipline and the paid sharing rollout. Neither the margin figure nor the year-over-year comparison is directly stated in the filing — both require calculation across the income statement.
>
> **Supporting Facts:**
> - Source: Netflix 10-K FY2023, pgs. 57, 59
> - Total revenue (FY2023): $33.7B (pg. 57)
> - Operating income (FY2023): $7.0B (pg. 57)
> - Total revenue (FY2022): $31.6B (pg. 59)
> - Operating income (FY2022): $5.6B (pg. 59)

## Hard (4 total, across documents)

Hard questions require drawing on information from at least 2 financial filings. You are not limited to the 3 filings in your task set — you may look up additional filings from other companies as needed (see `2.0_all_companies.csv` for a list). There is no upper limit on how many documents you draw from; stronger questions often use more. These questions should reveal broader patterns, comparisons, or trends across companies or time. They should not be narrowly specific to the three companies in your task set.

The more documents your answer draws from, the stronger the claim it can support. A question answered with 2 filings can compare two companies; one answered with 5 or more can make a sector-level claim or identify an outlier. Aim for the most informative question you can offer given your expertise.

**Filing date requirement:** All filings cited in Hard Q&As must be dated October 2023 or newer. Reviewers will verify this; submissions citing older filings will be rejected.

> **Note:** Page numbers in the examples below are illustrative — your submissions must include accurate page numbers from the actual filings.

Hard questions may be, but are not limited to:
- **Descriptive:** What consistently appears across filings of this type?
- **Comparative:** What varies meaningfully across companies?
- **Temporal:** How do macroeconomic events show up across filings over time?

**Example 1 (comparative):**

> **Q:** Among major energy companies, which firms systematically use financial derivatives to hedge commodity price exposure, and what does this reveal about their approach to risk management?
>
> **A:** Based on their most recent 10-K filings, ExxonMobil (FY2023) explicitly states it does not use financial derivatives to hedge commodity price risk, relying instead on its integrated business model as a natural hedge. In contrast, Sunrun (FY2023) discloses the use of interest rate swaps and other instruments to manage financing risk on its solar asset portfolios. Plug Power (FY2023) carries significant commodity and supply-chain exposure with limited hedging disclosure. This pattern suggests that larger integrated energy companies rely on operational diversification rather than financial instruments, while smaller capital-intensive firms are more exposed and less systematically hedged.
>
> **Supporting Facts:**
> - ExxonMobil 10-K FY2023, pg. 45: "The Company does not use financial derivative instruments to hedge commodity price risk, relying instead on its integrated business model as a natural hedge."
> - Sunrun 10-K FY2023, pg. 67: Discloses use of interest rate swaps and other instruments to manage financing risk on solar asset portfolios.
> - Plug Power 10-K FY2023: No hedging policy disclosed for commodity or supply-chain exposure. [No hedging policy found in risk factors or notes to financial statements; reviewed pgs. 38–52.]

**Example 2 (temporal):**

> **Q:** How did the rising interest rate environment of 2022–2023 manifest across 10-K risk factor disclosures for U.S. financial services companies?
>
> **A:** Across the 10-K filings of JPMorgan Chase (FY2023), Bank of America (FY2023), and Ally Financial (FY2023), rising interest rates appear prominently in risk factor and financial performance sections. JPMorgan disclosed net interest income of $89.3 billion in FY2023 (up from $66.3 billion in FY2022), attributing the increase to higher rates while also flagging duration risk in its securities portfolio. Bank of America reported similar NII growth but disclosed approximately $100 billion in unrealized losses on its held-to-maturity portfolio. Ally Financial, as a consumer auto lender, highlighted margin compression risk as deposit costs rose faster than loan yields. All three filings include explicit interest rate sensitivity analyses showing the projected impact of a +100 basis point parallel shift on net interest income. This pattern reflects a consistent industry-wide adjustment to a higher-rate regime, with each company's exposure shaped by its balance sheet composition.
>
> **Supporting Facts:**
> - JPMorgan Chase 10-K FY2023, pg. 163: Net interest income $89.3B (FY2023) vs. $66.3B (FY2022); duration risk in securities portfolio disclosed.
> - Bank of America 10-K FY2023, pg. 54: NII growth noted; approximately $100B in unrealized losses on held-to-maturity portfolio disclosed.
> - Ally Financial 10-K FY2023, pg. 47: Margin compression risk flagged as deposit costs rose faster than loan yields.
> - Interest rate sensitivity disclosures (+100bps NII scenario): JPMorgan Chase (pg. 171), Bank of America (pg. 61), Ally Financial (pg. 49).

## Supporting Facts

Every Q&A requires a Supporting Facts section. This makes each submission self-contained so that a reviewer can assess accuracy and evaluate your reasoning without opening the source documents. What the section must include varies by category:

- **Easy** — source citation with page number. The reviewer can open that page and confirm the fact is stated exactly as you claim.

  ```
  Supporting Facts:
    Source: Netflix 10-K FY2023, pg. 57
  ```

- **Medium** — source with page numbers, plus every raw input needed to verify any calculation. The reviewer should be able to run the arithmetic themselves using only the values you supply.

  ```
  Supporting Facts:
    Source: Netflix 10-K FY2023, pgs. 57, 59
    Total revenue (FY2023): $33.7B (pg. 57)
    Operating income (FY2023): $7.0B (pg. 57)
    Total revenue (FY2022): $31.6B (pg. 59)
    Operating income (FY2022): $5.6B (pg. 59)
  ```

- **Hard** — for each company cited, reproduce the specific data point or language the answer relies on, with filing and page number. A reviewer must be able to evaluate whether your cross-company synthesis is justified using only what you provide, without opening any source document.

  ```
  Supporting Facts:
    ExxonMobil 10-K FY2023, pg. 45: "The Company does not use financial derivative
      instruments to hedge commodity price risk..."
    Sunrun 10-K FY2023, pg. 67: Discloses use of interest rate swaps to manage
      financing risk on solar asset portfolios.
    Plug Power 10-K FY2023: No hedging policy found in risk factors or notes to
      financial statements. [Reviewed pgs. 38–52.]
  ```

  **Note on absent disclosures:** when your answer depends on the absence of something (e.g., "no going concern qualification"), document where you looked and confirm nothing was found:
  ```
  [No going concern qualification found in auditor's report or risk factors; reviewed pgs. 38–52.]
  ```

## Getting Started: Accessing Your Filings

You will receive a pre-filled CSV file listing your 3 assigned companies, their filing types, filing dates, SEC search links, and suggested PDF filenames. Before writing any Q&As, you must download the PDF for each assigned filing. Follow these steps for each company:

1. **Open the SEC search link** — Click the link in the `sec_url` column of your assignment CSV. This opens a Google search filtered to SEC.gov for that company and filing type.

2. **Find the correct filing** — Click through to the SEC EDGAR filing page for the most recent filing matching your assigned type (10-K or 10-Q) and filing date.

3. **Open as HTML** — On the SEC filing page, click "Menu" (top-left of the document viewer), then click "Open as HTML". Note the URL of the HTML version — this is your `sec_page_url` to record in your CSV.

4. **Save as PDF** — With the HTML filing open in your browser, press Ctrl+P (or Cmd+P on Mac) to open the print dialog. Set the destination to "Save as PDF", then click Save. Name the file using the filename already listed in your assignment CSV (e.g., `Apple_10K_2025.pdf`). Save it to a folder you can easily locate.

5. **Repeat for all 3 assigned companies** — You should end up with 3 PDF files before you begin writing Q&As.

**Deliverables — what to return when you are done:**
1. Your completed CSV (all 10 rows filled in: question, answer, supporting_facts, additional_info, and source_1_pdf_filename confirmed)
2. The 3 PDF files you downloaded (named exactly as listed in your CSV)

For Hard questions you may consult additional filings beyond your 3 assigned companies. If you do, download those PDFs as well and include them in your supporting_facts citations.

## Quality Standards

**1. Factual only.** Questions must ask for information supported by the documents. Do not ask for opinions unless an opinion is explicitly stated in the filing.

**2. Supporting Facts.** Every Q&A must include a Supporting Facts section after the answer. See the Supporting Facts section above for requirements by category.

**3. No PII.** Do not include personal names, contact information, or other identifying details of individuals in your questions or answers. If a filing mentions an executive by name in a way that is incidental to the financial data, paraphrase or refer to the role ("the CEO") rather than the individual. Reviewers will flag and reject PII; catching it yourself avoids a revision round.

**4. Additional Info section.** If your answer depends on context not obvious from the Q&A itself — such as an industry convention, a non-standard definition, or a methodological assumption — add a brief "Additional Info:" note at the end. This is separate from Supporting Facts, which covers evidentiary sourcing.

> **Example:**
> Additional Info: "Free cash flow" is calculated here as operating cash flow minus capital expenditures, consistent with Boeing's investor relations disclosures. The 10-K does not use this term directly.
